Books from Richard Feynman

Richard Feynman was a prolific author and he wrote or co-wrote numerous books throughout his career. Here are some of his most notable works:

"Space-Time Approach to Quantum Electrodynamics" (1949)

- In this paper, Feynman introduced his "Feynman diagrams," a graphical method for calculating the behavior of subatomic particles in electromagnetic fields. This paper laid the groundwork for quantum electrodynamics (QED), which is a theory of the interaction of matter with electromagnetic fields.

"The Theory of Positrons" (1949)

- In this paper, Feynman developed a theory of the behavior of positrons (the antimatter counterparts of electrons) based on QED. This work was important for the development of the standard model of particle physics.

"The Principle of Least Action in Quantum Mechanics" (1948)

- In this paper, Feynman developed a new formulation of quantum mechanics based on the principle of least action. This formulation, known as the path integral formulation, is now a standard method for calculating the behavior of quantum systems.

"A Space-Time Approach to Non-Relativistic Quantum Mechanics" (1950)

- In this paper, Feynman extended his space-time approach to quantum mechanics to include non-relativistic systems. This work was important for the development of the path integral formulation of quantum mechanics.

"The Feynman Lectures on Physics" (1963-65)

- This is a three-volume set of textbooks based on the lectures that Feynman gave to undergraduate students at the California Institute of Technology. The books cover a wide range of topics in physics, from classical mechanics to quantum mechanics and relativity.

"QED: The Strange Theory of Light and Matter" (1985)

- This book presents Feynman's interpretation of quantum electrodynamics, one of the most successful theories in the history of physics. Feynman explains the behavior of subatomic particles in electromagnetic fields using his famous Feynman diagrams.

"Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!" (1985)

- This book is a collection of anecdotes and stories from Feynman's life, covering topics ranging from his experiences working on the Manhattan Project to his love of music and art.

  • "What Do You Care What Other People Think?" (1988) - This book is a sequel to "Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!" and features more stories from Feynman's life, including his experiences investigating the Challenger space shuttle disaster.

    • "The Character of Physical Law" (1965)

    - This book is based on a series of lectures that Feynman gave at Cornell University, in which he discusses the fundamental principles of physics and the nature of scientific inquiry.

    "The Meaning of It All: Thoughts of a Citizen-Scientist" (1998)

    - This book is a collection of three lectures that Feynman gave at the University of Washington, in which he discusses the role of science in society and the responsibilities of scientists.
